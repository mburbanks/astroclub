Some of our Pictures



Orange Setting Moon, 2340 19th July 2026.
An extremely deep orange setting Moon, and it really was that orange to the eye caused by wildfire-smoke in the atmosphere filtering/scattering out the blue end of the spectrum, taken near midnight on 19th July 2026. Unusual because it's rare here to see any celestial object all the way down to the horizon without serious almospheric attentuation. Also lucky to see it set into the sea, rather than the land which occupies most of my horizon-vista. Looking West over Sherkin Island, with the Jolly Roger pub and the former Sherkin Hotel brightly lit.
The second picture, taken 8 or so minutes later, shows the now highly-distorted Moon partially set into the sea-horizon. At this point, geometrically, the Moon (its centre-point) is in fact 1 full degree below the horizon. Lunar diameter being around 1 degree, it should be invisible. But atmospheric refraction allows it to appear an estimated 0.3 degrees above the horizon. This is why precise observable setting-times for celestial objects cannot ever be truly accurate.
Both taken from Ballylynch (Magnus Burbanks).
Canon EOS 7Dmk2 + EF 300mm F/2.8L. 6 seconds at f/4.0 ISO 1600.


Lunar Eclipse, about half an hour from totality, 6:00am 14th March 2025.
There was plenty of cloud around which completely obscured it thereafter. The classic Total Lunar Eclipse photo shows a full deep-orange disc. But this is possibly more interesting: it captures all the elements in a single image: Earth's shadow, separating the more-illuminated region in the Penumbra and the dimmer part within the full Umbra (the dimmer part illuminated by sunlight diffracted ("bent") around Earth); and a hint of the orange colour starting to appear as the return light from lower part has more of the blue filtered out by Earth's thicker-lower-down atmosphere. This is a single image (i.e. not a composite) and not re-touched.
Taken from Ballylynch (Magnus Burbanks).
Canon EOS 7Dmk2 + EF 400mm F/2.8L + 1.4x converter.
